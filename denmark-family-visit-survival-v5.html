1) Warm-up — You, your trip, your family

Start with the questions and answers you really need for your trip.

How to ask questions

Question word + auxiliary + subject + verb

Where do you live? What would you like to visit? When should we meet?

Yes / No question = auxiliary + subject + verb

Do you like Danish food? Can we meet after lunch? Would you like some tea?

Answer pattern

I live in France. I’m going to Denmark next week. I’d like to visit the coast. We could meet in the afternoon.

Model answer

I live in France. I’m going to Denmark to visit my son and my daughter-in-law. I’d like to spend time with my family, visit beautiful places, and practise my English with them.

  • Where do you live?
  • Why are you going to Denmark?
  • Who are you going to see?
  • What would you like to visit?
  • When should we meet tomorrow?

Choose the auxiliary

What ___ you like to visit?

Choose the best answer

3) Prepositions lesson — place, movement, time, plans

This lesson helps you explain why we say in Denmark but go to Denmark.

Rule 1 — in for position / place

Use in when a person or thing is inside a place or when you talk about location.

I live in France. My son lives in Denmark. We are in the kitchen. There are beautiful beaches in Denmark.

Rule 2 — to after movement verbs

Use to after many movement verbs: go, come, drive, walk, travel, return, fly.

I’m going to Denmark. We can drive to the coast. I’d like to travel to a nature reserve.

Very useful contrast

I am in Denmark. = position / location now

I am going to Denmark. = movement / destination

Other useful prepositions

  • at for a point: at the airport, at home, at 10 o’clock
  • on for days and surfaces: on Monday, on the table
  • with for company: with my son, with my daughter-in-law
  • for for purpose / duration: I’m here for a week. This gift is for you.
  • after / before for order: after breakfast, before lunch
  • near for close position: near the sea, near their house

I live in France, but I’m going to Denmark next week. I’m staying with my son and my daughter-in-law for a few days. We could meet at the station at 11 o’clock and go to the coast after lunch.

Choose the correct prepositions

I live France but I’m going Denmark.

Time and company

Order and place

4) Adjectives and adverbs lesson — clear rules + useful vocabulary

Use adjectives to describe people, places, and things. Use adverbs to describe how someone does something.

Rule A — adjective

An adjective describes a noun.

a beautiful city • a kind daughter-in-law • a quiet place • a friendly welcome

After be, use an adjective, not an adverb.

Denmark is beautiful. She is kind. The house is quiet.

Useful adjectives

  • beautiful = beau / belle
  • friendly = amical / chaleureux
  • quiet = calme
  • helpful = serviable
  • interesting = intéressant
  • peaceful = paisible

Rule B — adverb

An adverb describes a verb: how someone speaks, drives, explains, walks, answers...

She speaks clearly. He drives carefully. They welcomed me warmly.

Many adverbs are formed with adjective + -ly.

clear → clearly • careful → carefully • warm → warmly • polite → politely • slow → slowly

Important irregular form: good → well

She is a good cook. She cooks well.

Useful adverbs

  • carefully = avec soin
  • clearly = clairement
  • politely = poliment
  • slowly = lentement
  • warmly = chaleureusement
  • well = bien

Adjective or adverb?

My daughter-in-law is very ___.

How does she speak?

She explains things very ___.

Choose the right form

Denmark is and people often speak very .

5) Guided dialogues — practical and natural

Each dialogue starts with useful language. First read the support box. Then practise the dialogue.

Useful language before you speak

  • It’s lovely to see you.
  • Thank you for having me.
  • How have you been?
  • Your home is beautiful.
  • Did you have a good week?

Dialogue

You: It’s lovely to see you. Thank you for having me.
Daughter-in-law: We’re so happy you’re here. How was your trip?
You: It was good, thank you. I’m a little tired, but I’m very happy to be here.

Useful language before you speak

  • What would you recommend?
  • I’d like to visit the coast / a nature reserve / the town centre.
  • When should we meet?
  • We could go in the morning / after lunch / in the afternoon.
  • That sounds lovely.

Dialogue

Daughter-in-law: What would you like to do tomorrow?
You: I’d like to visit a beautiful place in nature. What would you recommend?
Daughter-in-law: We could go to the coast in the morning.
You: That sounds lovely. What time should we meet?

Useful language before you speak

  • I work as an assessor with young people in court.
  • I listen, observe, and assess situations.
  • My work is interesting but sometimes emotional.
  • I wanted to make a positive difference.

Dialogue

Daughter-in-law: Could you tell me a little about your work?
You: Yes, of course. I work as an assessor with young people in court.
Daughter-in-law: That sounds very important.
You: It is. The work is sometimes difficult, but it is meaningful.

Useful language before you speak

  • I wanted to volunteer at an animal shelter.
  • There were already too many volunteers.
  • I still love animals and I care a lot about their welfare.
  • I enjoy visiting places with nature and wildlife.

Dialogue

Daughter-in-law: Do you still do any volunteer work with animals?
You: I wanted to volunteer at an animal shelter, but there were already too many volunteers.
Daughter-in-law: That must have been disappointing.
You: Yes, a little, but I still love animals and I enjoy visiting places where I can see nature and wildlife.

6) Interactive roleplay — a conversation you can really use

Imagine you are having breakfast with your son and your daughter-in-law. Then you start planning the day together.

Situation

You are at breakfast. You want to be friendly, ask questions, and suggest a visit.

Useful language

  • Did you sleep well?
  • What’s the plan for today?
  • I’d love to visit somewhere peaceful.
  • Would it be better to go in the morning or in the afternoon?
  • We could meet after breakfast and leave at 10.

Your roleplay prompt

Start the conversation. Greet your daughter-in-law warmly, ask about the plan for the day, say what you would like to visit, and ask when you should meet. Then answer one question about your work and one question about the animal shelter.

7) Writing practice

Use the guided writing first. Then build your own final text.

Writing task 1

Write a short message to your daughter-in-law. Thank her for welcoming you, say what you would like to visit, and suggest a time to meet.

Thank you for welcoming me to your home. I’m very happy to be here. I’d love to visit a beautiful place in nature while I’m in Denmark. If it suits you, we could meet after breakfast and leave at around 10 o’clock.

Writing task 2

Write 4–5 sentences to explain your work and your interest in animal welfare.

I work as an assessor with young people in court. The work is meaningful and sometimes emotional. I wanted to volunteer at an animal shelter because I love animals very much. However, there were already too many volunteers. I still enjoy talking about animal welfare and visiting natural places.
