Part 1

Ordinals: first, second, third...

When do you use ordinals?

Use ordinals for position, sequence, ranking, dates and order details.

Position: This is the first order today.

Sequence: The second tray is vegetarian.

Date: The delivery is on the twenty-fifth of June.

Ranking: Food safety is the first priority.

FR : Les ordinaux servent à dire “premier, deuxième, troisième...” pour l’ordre, les dates et les priorités.

How to write vs how to say dates

Write25 June
June 25
25th June
Saythe twenty-fifth of June
June twenty-fifth

Do not say: twenty-five June. Use the ordinal sound: twenty-fifth.

Quick ordinal list

1st first · 2nd second · 3rd third · 4th fourth · 5th fifth · 8th eighth · 9th ninth · 12th twelfth · 20th twentieth · 21st twenty-first · 25th twenty-fifth · 30th thirtieth

Mini writing practice

  • The first...

  • On the twenty-fifth of...
  • My first priority is...

Part 2

Quantities and frequency

Each / Every

Each = one by one, individual focus.

Every = all, general group focus.

Each meal has a label. = meal 1, meal 2, meal 3...

Every order is important. = all orders in general.

FR : each = chaque élément individuellement. every = tous les éléments d’un groupe.

Some / Any

Some = positive sentences, offers and requests.

Any = questions and negatives.

Some clients ask for special meals.

Do you have any allergies?

We don’t have any changes.

FR : some = des / quelques. any = en question ou négation.

Much / Many / A lot of

Many = countable plural.

Much = uncountable, often questions/negatives.

A lot of = many or much, more natural in speaking.

many meals · many labels

much time · much information

a lot of work · a lot of orders

All / All the

All = general idea.

All the = specific group.

All clients want good service.

All the meals are labelled.

Always / All the time

Always = every time, normal frequency word.

All the time = very often, sometimes with emotion.

Food safety is always important.

The phone rings all the time today.

A few / A little

A few = small number, countable plural.

A little = small quantity, uncountable.

a few orders · a few labels

a little time · a little information

Part 3

TO / AT: movement, recipient or place?

TO = movement / destination / recipient

I am going to the airport.

We deliver the food to the airline.

I will send the confirmation to the client.

We are moving to Lisbon.

AT = exact place / position / time

I am at the airport.

The driver is at the gate.

We meet at 10 a.m.

I work at the restaurant today.

Easy rule: TO = direction / recipient. AT = exact location / time.

Part 4

Comparisons and family words

Vocabulary by category

Select a category. Read the translation and example. Then use the words in the exercises.

Short adjective

adjective + -er / the + adjective + -est

hard → harder → the hardest

old → older → the oldest

fast → faster → the fastest

Not: more hard. Say: harder.

Long adjective

more + adjective / the most + adjective

difficult → more difficult → the most difficult

important → more important → the most important

professional → more professional → the most professional

Older / oldest

Use for people, things and general comparison.

My daughter is older now.

This building is older than the airport.

This is the oldest restaurant in the area.

Elder / eldest

Use mainly for family, before a noun. It is more formal.

my elder brother · my eldest daughter

Natural everyday English: my older daughter. More formal family English: my eldest daughter.

Realistic scenario: updated aircraft order

An airline client changes a crew breakfast order. One meal must be ketogenic, one meal has allergies, and each item needs a clear English label. The food must be delivered to the aircraft at 07:45. The aircraft has a small oven but no microwave. Write a professional message to confirm the details and ask for the exact delivery point.

Your writing task

Write 8–10 sentences. Use:

  • one ordinal: first, second, third, twenty-fifth...
  • two quantity words: each, every, some, any, a lot of, many, much...
  • to and at correctly;
  • one comparison: harder, more difficult, the most important...
  • three connectors: first, then, after that, however, because, so, finally...
  • one professional question.
