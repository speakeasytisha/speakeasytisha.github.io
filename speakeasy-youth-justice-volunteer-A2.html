3) Volunteer procedure — helping juveniles (France focus)

This add-on explains how volunteering typically works (onboarding, rules, safety) and gives you language to describe your role clearly for the CLOE exam (A2 → A2.2).

A) Common pathways (examples)

1) PJJ reserve — citizens support educational / reintegration activities with professionals.

2) Lay assessor (tribunal for children) — a volunteer citizen who sits with the judge and takes an oath.

3) Delegate of the prosecutor — a citizen (or association) authorised to carry out tasks under the prosecutor’s control.

4) Associations — mentoring + reintegration support (always with a framework).

Different cities/organisations have different requirements. Always follow your supervisor’s rules.

Exam tip: use I’m trained to… / I work under supervision / It’s confidential.
B) Safe onboarding checklist (typical steps)
  1. Apply (form + motivation).
  2. Interview (availability, skills, boundaries).
  3. Checks (identity + background check).
  4. Training (youth, communication, safety).
  5. Confidentiality (rules + what you can/can’t share).
  6. Start with supervision + debrief after sessions.
Golden rule: you can be kind and firm. You support progress — you don’t “save” someone alone.
C) How to help (practical + safe)

Do: listen, stay calm, set clear limits, encourage small steps, follow up.

Don’t: promise secrets, give legal advice, meet alone without the framework, or share names/details.

If there is risk (violence, self-harm, abuse): tell your supervisor immediately.

  • Build trust and keep a calm tone.
  • Help with goals: school, training, daily routine.
  • Encourage accountability: “What can you do next time?”
  • Work with a team (educator, supervisor, court contact).
Language focus: support, encourage, follow up, set limits.
