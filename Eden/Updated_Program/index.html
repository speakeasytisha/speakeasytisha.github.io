Core direction

This is not designed as a general writing course. The focus is executive oral communication: clarity, impact, structure, attention management, physical presence, intentional energy variation, voice, and leadership presence in English.

01 · Positioning

Your professional context

The plan is built around your role as an aeronautical and aerospace engineer and project manager working in one of the world’s most safety-critical industries.

◎

Your technical responsibility

You are responsible for verifying that components, systems, and equipment meet customer requirements, comply with applicable safety standards, and remain fully traceable.

▣

Your safety environment

Your products are part of one of the world’s most safety-critical industries, where precision, compliance, traceability, risk control, and operational reliability are essential.

⇄

Your leadership scope

You coordinate several specialist teams, including propulsion systems, landing gear systems, equipment teams, and other technical workstreams. Each team must verify its own scope and contribute to safe, compliant, and reliable operation.

▲

Your communication challenge

You need to defend projects and recommendations in front of management and senior stakeholders who have limited time and expect a clear, decision-oriented message quickly.

02 · Continuity

What you have already worked on

The next phase should continue from the work already completed. The goal is not to restart from the beginning, but to stabilise and apply your existing tools in higher-stakes aerospace and senior management situations.

01

Message structure

You have worked on the Golden Circle logic: WHY → HOW → WHAT, also expressed as intention, direction, and content.

02

One-sentence discipline

You have practised reducing over-explanation, avoiding circular speech, and identifying the one clear sentence that carries the message.

03

Voice, pace, pauses, and presence

You have worked on speaking more slowly, using silence intentionally, lowering the pressure in your voice, and aligning posture with authority.

04

Frame holding and interaction control

You have practised staying calm when interrupted, recovering attention, responding without over-justifying, and guiding the interaction back to the objective.

05

Preparation for executive-level situations

You have begun connecting these tools to situations such as senior management communication, decision-making, Executive Committee / CODIR scenarios, and high-stakes project updates.

03 · Training mission

The main objective

By the end of the training, you should be able to express complex aerospace project information in English with executive clarity: a precise message, a clear structure, controlled delivery, convincing reasoning, and the ability to capture, hold, and recover the audience’s attention when time is limited.

The training should focus on

  • Pitching safety-critical projects clearly
  • Structuring your message before speaking
  • Capturing attention quickly
  • Speaking to senior management with impact
  • Handling questions and objections
  • Using voice, rhythm, pauses, emphasis, and energy variation
  • Using posture, gaze, gesture, and camera presence intentionally
  • Convincing senior stakeholders through relevance, evidence, impact, and a clear recommendation
  • Preparing for the CLOE oral interview through professional scenarios

The training should avoid

  • A general English course disconnected from your job
  • A writing-focused programme
  • Long grammar lessons without oral application
  • Generic communication examples unrelated to aerospace engineering, safety, or project leadership
  • Correcting every detail at the expense of confidence and fluency

04 · Confirmed priorities

Your feedback has shaped the next phase

The programme now gives additional emphasis to the priorities you identified as most valuable: attention management, voice and authority, physical presence and energy variation, and influencing decision-makers.

01

Attention capture and recovery

You will practise opening with a clear purpose, recognising early signs that the audience is drifting, and refocusing attention without becoming defensive or over-explaining.

  • Open with the issue, consequence, or decision required.
  • Use questions and signposts to guide attention.
  • Recover focus with calm, concise redirecting language.
02

Physical presence and energy variation

You will learn to vary posture, movement, gaze, pace, gesture, and vocal energy with intention. The goal is not to “perform”, but to make the message easier to follow and more credible.

  • Use calm, steady delivery for risk, analysis, difficult questions, and key decisions.
  • Use more energy for openings, urgency, critical outcomes, transitions, and calls to action.
  • Use stillness and pauses when authority matters more than intensity.
03

Convincing senior stakeholders

You will focus on convincing through clarity, relevance, evidence, risk awareness, and a practical recommendation — not through excessive detail or volume.

  • Connect the technical issue to business, safety, timing, and operational impact.
  • Show the logic behind your recommendation.
  • Make the requested decision or commitment explicit.

05 · Proposed route

Training modules for the remaining hours

Each module is designed as an oral, practical, aerospace-oriented communication lab, with light written support only when it helps you prepare a stronger spoken message. Modules on attention management and voice/presence receive particular emphasis.

Module 1

Alignment and oral diagnostic

Confirm your priorities, your real professional situations, and the communication habits you want to change.

  • Review your previous work and current priorities.
  • Identify your most important speaking situations.
  • Record a short baseline pitch to compare progress later.
  • Define one concrete target: senior management pitch, Executive Committee / CODIR update, technical recommendation, or CLOE oral task.
Module 2

Executive message architecture

Move from technical explanation to executive clarity: issue, impact, recommendation, decision.

  • Build a short structure before speaking.
  • Separate essential information from technical detail.
  • Use clear signposting: “The key point is…”, “The impact is…”, “What I recommend is…”
  • Practise speaking in one clear line of reasoning instead of a circular explanation.
Module 3

Aerospace project pitch

Present a safety-critical aerospace project or recommendation in 30 seconds, 1 minute, and 3 minutes.

  • Start with why the issue matters now.
  • Explain requirements compliance, non-conformity risk, safety impact, and operational consequences without overloading the audience.
  • Adapt the same project pitch for specialist technical teams, senior management, and a time-limited decision-making audience.
  • End with a clear expected decision or next step.
Module 4

Attention capture and attention recovery

Learn how to open strongly, keep focus, and recover attention when the audience disengages or the discussion becomes too detailed.

  • Use opening sentences that immediately clarify the issue, the risk, and the decision needed.
  • Use strategic questions, changes in pace, and concise signposts to guide attention.
  • Recognise when an audience is drifting, losing focus, or moving too quickly into technical detail.
  • Redirect with calm, precise sentences: “Let’s come back to the decision we need.”
  • Practise how to regain engagement without raising your voice or over-explaining.
Module 5

Questions, objections, and interruptions

Stay structured when challenged, interrupted, or pushed into excessive technical detail.

  • Pause before answering.
  • Respond to the intention, not the pressure.
  • Clarify without over-justifying.
  • Use diplomatic but firm framing: “Let me separate the technical point from the decision we need.”
Module 6

Voice, physical presence, energy, rhythm, and authority

Use voice, posture, gaze, movement, pauses, and energy variation to make your message more credible, engaging, and easier to follow.

  • Work on word stress, sentence stress, professional rhythm, and intelligibility.
  • Practise when calm delivery is more persuasive: risk, analysis, difficult questions, and key decisions.
  • Practise when more energy is useful: openings, urgency, transitions, critical outcomes, and calls to action.
  • Use posture, gaze, and controlled gesture to support authority in a room and on camera.
  • Strengthen the pronunciation of aerospace, compliance, safety, and leadership vocabulary.
Module 7

Convincing and influencing decision-makers

Turn technical expertise into a persuasive, decision-oriented message for senior stakeholders.

  • Connect technical evidence to safety, business, programme, timing, and operational consequences.
  • Build a clear line of reasoning: issue, evidence, impact, recommendation, decision.
  • Adapt the level of detail to the audience without weakening technical credibility.
  • Practise confident calls to action and recommendation language.
Module 8

CLOE oral integration

Prepare for the CLOE oral interview through realistic professional speaking tasks, not school-style drills.

  • Introduce yourself and your role clearly.
  • Explain your professional responsibilities in aerospace engineering and project leadership.
  • Handle a professional role-play.
  • Discuss a work-related topic with clarity, interaction, and natural reformulation.

07 · Practice scenarios

Suggested professional simulations

These scenarios can be adjusted with your real project examples while keeping confidential information protected.

Scenario 1 · Pitching a technical recommendation

You have 90 seconds to present a recommendation about component compliance, safety impact, operational risk, and the decision required from management.

Target opening: “The key issue today is not only component compliance. It is the level of operational risk we are willing to accept.”

Scenario 2 · Explaining risk without overloading the audience

You need to explain why a component or system needs further verification, while keeping the message clear for a non-specialist audience.

Target opening: “Before going into the detail, I would like to clarify the risk and the decision we need.”

Scenario 3 · Coordinating several technical teams

You need to align propulsion systems, landing gear systems, equipment teams, and other technical workstreams around shared compliance and safety objectives.

Target opening: “Each team has its own technical scope, but the objective is shared: safe, compliant, and reliable operation.”

Scenario 4 · Speaking to management with limited time

You have three minutes with senior stakeholders. You must capture attention, prioritise the message, use calm authority, and make the expected decision or next step clear.

Target opening: “I will focus on three points: the current risk, the recommended action, and the decision needed today.”

Scenario 5 · Convincing through evidence and energy variation

You need to persuade senior stakeholders to support a recommendation. You will vary your presence deliberately: a higher-energy opening to establish relevance, a calm evidence-based explanation for the risk, and a clear, confident call to action.

Target sequence: “This decision matters now because it affects our exposure to risk. The evidence is clear. My recommendation is to proceed with the additional verification phase.”
