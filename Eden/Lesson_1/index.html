01 · Flight plan

What you will practise today

This lesson is designed to make your spoken message more structured, concise and convincing when you speak to senior management in a safety-critical aerospace context.

Cette leçon vise à rendre votre message oral plus structuré, concis et convaincant lorsque vous vous adressez à une direction dans un contexte aéronautique critique.

1

Clarify

Identify the decision-level message before giving technical details.

Identifier le message décisionnel avant les détails techniques.

2

Convince

Connect your message to risk, evidence, impact and recommendation.

Relier le message au risque, aux preuves, à l’impact et à la recommandation.

3

Control attention

Use openings, voice, pauses, posture and energy variation intentionally.

Utiliser les ouvertures, la voix, les pauses, la posture et l’énergie avec intention.

Core communication principle

In aerospace, technical accuracy is essential. In executive communication, accuracy must be organised. Senior management needs to hear the issue, the risk, the impact, your recommendation and the decision required.

En aéronautique, la précision technique est essentielle. Dans la communication avec la direction, cette précision doit être organisée. La direction doit entendre l’enjeu, le risque, l’impact, votre recommandation et la décision attendue.

“Your goal is not to explain everything. Your goal is to make the right decision easier.”

03 · Navigation system

The executive message structure

Use this structure when you need to speak clearly to senior management with limited time.

How to use this structure

Start with the decision or the key point. Add the reason, the risk and the recommendation. Finish with the impact or the action needed. If the message becomes too long, return to the first question: What decision does the audience need to make?

Commencez par la décision ou le point clé. Ajoutez la raison, le risque et la recommandation. Terminez par l’impact ou l’action attendue.

05 · Baseline task

Your 60-second professional presentation

Step 1 — Speak naturally

Present your role in 60 seconds. Include your responsibilities, the teams you coordinate, the safety-critical nature of your work and the kind of message you need to bring to management.

  • Job title and scope
  • Teams or systems coordinated
  • Compliance, customer requirements and safety
  • Why communication matters

Step 2 — Short model

Use this if the first version is too long.

I am an aerospace project manager. I coordinate technical teams and make sure components and systems meet customer requirements, safety standards and operational reliability expectations. My communication role is to make complex technical information clear enough for management to make the right decision.

Step 3 — Polished model

Use this after the structure is clear.

I am an aerospace project manager responsible for coordinating several technical teams, including propulsion systems, landing gear systems and equipment scopes. My role is to ensure that components and systems meet customer requirements, comply with safety standards and operate reliably. Because this is one of the world’s most safety-critical industries, communication must be clear, precise and decision-oriented. When I speak to management, I need to transform complex technical information into a message that is easy to understand, easy to trust and easy to act on.

Notes — how to improve the answer

Clarity

The audience should understand your role and responsibility without needing technical background.

Sentence length

Strong executive speech uses shorter sentences. One idea = one sentence.

Circular explanation

A circular answer repeats the same idea without moving forward. Move from role → risk → impact → decision.

Rhythm

A controlled rhythm includes pauses after key ideas. Fast speech often sounds less senior.

Posture

A stable posture supports the voice. Avoid unnecessary movement when making the main point.

Decision focus

The message should help management understand what needs to be decided or prioritised.

06 · Voice lab

Energy variation: capture attention, build trust, recommend

Practice sentence: “The main risk is a compliance gap before the next milestone.”

Capture attention

When: at the beginning, when introducing the key risk, or when attention is dropping.

How: slightly higher energy, clean first word, clear direction, no rushing.

A speaker should be energetic when the audience needs to immediately understand that a point matters. Energy is useful for opening, signalling urgency and bringing attention back.

Build trust

When: when explaining risk, safety, uncertainty or sensitive information.

How: slower pace, lower tone, more space between ideas.

Calm is more efficient when the topic is complex or sensitive. It makes the speaker sound controlled, credible and reliable.

Recommend

When: when giving your position or asking for a decision.

How: firm, stable, with a pause before the final idea.

A recommendation needs firm energy: not aggressive, not hesitant. The voice should make the direction feel stable.

Your turn

Record the same sentence three times: once to capture attention, once to build trust and once to recommend. Then listen back and compare the intention.

Notes — what to listen for
  • Too fast: the sentence sounds urgent but less controlled.
  • Too flat: the message may be accurate but does not capture attention.
  • Too soft: the risk may sound less important than it is.
  • Strong version: the first words are clear, the pace is controlled and the final idea lands.

07 · Physical presence

Position, posture and intentional energy

1

Anchor

Stand or sit with a stable base. Stability helps the voice sound more controlled.

2

Deliver one thought

Say one sentence without moving unnecessarily. Let the sentence finish before adding more information.

3

Pause

Pause for two seconds after the key point. Silence gives the audience time to process the message.

4

Shift energy

Use more energy to open, calmer energy to explain risk and firm energy to recommend.

Energy map

3/10 Calm control5/10 Firm authority7/10 Attention capture

Rule: Do not stay at the same energy level for the whole message. Change energy when the purpose changes.

Practice

Say this mini-message with three energy shifts:

“The decision we need today is simple. The risk is a compliance gap before the next milestone. My recommendation is to validate now.”

11 · CLOE transfer

From executive communication to certification success

CLOE-style oral question

Question: Can you describe your role and explain why communication is important in your job?

I am an aerospace project manager. I coordinate several technical teams and make sure that components and systems meet customer requirements, comply with safety standards and operate reliably. My role is to make the risks, priorities and recommendations easy to understand.
