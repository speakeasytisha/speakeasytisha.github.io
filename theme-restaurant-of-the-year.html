Restaurant of the Year – Burdell

Practise how to talk about a special restaurant: ask for a recommendation, understand the menu, describe the atmosphere and write a clear review in the past simple.

1. Vocabulary – recommendations & reservations

These words help you talk to the hotel, ask for a recommendation and book a table at a restaurant.

recommendation

a suggestion or advice about what you should choose

reservation

a table you book in advance so the restaurant keeps a place for you

cuisine

a style of cooking, like Italian, Japanese or soul food

tip

extra money you leave for the staff to thank them for the service (in the U.S., usually around 18–20% in a sit-down restaurant)

credit card

a plastic card used to pay instead of cash

confirmation

an email or message that says your reservation is accepted

2. Reading – Why is Burdell “Restaurant of the Year”?

Read about Burdell, then answer the questions. Notice how the text mixes facts (where, who, what) and opinion (why people love it).

RESTAURANT IMAGE HERE

Burdell is a modern soul food restaurant in the Temescal neighbourhood of Oakland, California. It is named after the chef’s grandmother, and the décor feels like a warm family home from the 1960s and 1970s, with vintage plates, old-school music and family photos.

The chef-owner worked in fine-dining restaurants before opening Burdell. He uses classic Black American comfort recipes, but he combines them with local California ingredients and modern techniques. The result is “California soul food”: creative dishes that still feel generous and familiar.

The restaurant is very popular, and tables are often booked weeks in advance. A major food magazine even called Burdell “Restaurant of the Year”, which brought visitors from all over the United States.

2. Why is the restaurant called “Burdell”?

3. What makes Burdell’s food special?

3. Grammar & style – Past simple in reviews

When you write a review about a restaurant you visited, use the past simple: was / were / went / ordered / tried / didn’t like / loved. You can also add adjectives and linking words to make the text more fluent.

Examples:

  • Last weekend, I had dinner at Burdell in Oakland.
  • The atmosphere was warm and cosy, with vintage décor and soul music.
  • We ordered several dishes to share, and everything was delicious.
  • The staff were friendly and professional, and we left a generous tip.

In reviews, we normally talk about a finished visit, so we prefer: was / were / went / ordered (past) and not is / are / go / order (present).
