1) Present Simple vs Present Continuous

Key rules

  • Present simple = habits, routines, general truths, timetables: My kids go to school at 8:15.
  • Present continuous = “right now”, temporary situations, changing trends: I’m filling out school forms right now.
  • Some verbs are often not continuous (state verbs): know, like, want, need, believe… → I know the address.
Aide (FR): Le présent simple = habitudes / généralités. Le présent continu = “en ce moment” / situation temporaire. Attention aux state verbs (know, like, want, need…) : on dit plutôt “I need…” (pas “I’m needing…”).
Present simple is for routines, habits, facts, and schedules. Present continuous is for actions happening now and temporary situations. Use the meaning: routine versus right now. Be careful with state verbs like know, like, want, and need.

2) Present Continuous for the Future (arranged plans)

When to use it

  • Use present continuous for personal arrangements (often already planned): We’re meeting the school counselor on Tuesday.
  • It usually includes a future time: tomorrow, next week, on Friday…
  • Contrast:
    • Will = spontaneous decision / prediction: OK, I’ll call them now.
    • Going to = intention or evidence: I’m going to apply for a driver’s license soon.
    • Present simple = timetables: The flight leaves at 7:10.
Aide (FR): Present continuous pour un plan déjà organisé (RDV, réservation, planning). “Will” = décision sur le moment (“OK, I’ll…”). Present simple = horaires officiels (train/vol/école).
Use the present continuous for arranged plans: meetings, appointments, and scheduled personal plans. Use will for decisions made now, going to for intentions, and present simple for official timetables.

Concrete examples (moving with kids)

Arranged plan
We’re touring the school tomorrow.
Timetable
The school bus arrives at 7:55.
Decision now
I forgot the form — I’ll email them.
Intention
We’re going to look for a pediatrician.

3) Present Perfect (and common French-native errors)

How it works

  • Form: have/has + past participle → I have lived, She has worked
  • Use it when the past connects to now:
    • Life experience: I’ve visited Boston.
    • Unfinished time: This week, we’ve met two neighbors.
    • Started in the past and still true: I’ve lived in France for 20 years.
    • Recent result: We’ve just received the school email.
  • Past simple is for a finished time (yesterday, in 2019, last week): We moved in 2019.
Aide (FR): Present perfect = passé relié au présent (expérience, “depuis”, “ce matin/aujourd’hui”, résultat récent). Erreur fréquente : “I am here since…” → “I have been here since…” et “Yesterday I have…” → “Yesterday I …” (past simple).
Present perfect is have or has plus past participle. Use it for experience, unfinished time, actions that started in the past and continue now, and recent results. Use past simple with finished time expressions like yesterday, last week, or in 2019.

Common French-native mistakes

❌ I am here since 2010.
✅ I have been here since 2010.
❌ Yesterday, we have met the principal.
✅ Yesterday, we met the principal.
❌ I know her since three years.
✅ I have known her for three years.
❌ I live in France for 20 years.
✅ I have lived in France for 20 years.
